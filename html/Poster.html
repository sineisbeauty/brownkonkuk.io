Effect on tumor growth in systems of two different types of tumor-associated neutrophils: A mathematical model

Glioblastoma, one of the deadly cancers with low survival rates derived from high recurrence and metastasis rate, have been treated in difficulty due to anatomical features of the human brain structure. Neutrophils, one of the anticancer agents in tumor microenvironment, have been regarded as a breakthrough of this situation because of its effective performance in the brain tissue; however, their usages for clinics are in hesitate due to controversial reports from studies: both tumor-promoting and tumor-inhibiting effects are reported. In this study, we build a mathematical model of interaction among the TME including tumor cells and two different tumor-associated neutrophils: the anti-tumorigenic N1 and the tumorigenic N2. Using neutrophil-related cytokines such as IFN-β and TGF-β, and antibody which selectively eliminates N2 neutrophils, we investigate dynamical variances among two competing neutrophils under different stimuli conditions and see how tumor cells grow according to these situations. To apply the model to the glioblastoma therapy, we develop a simulation which includes partial differential equations (PDEs) and a hybrid method approach to examine a spatial operation of tumor cells under temporal progression in the complex human brain anatomy.

Machine Learning of Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transitions during Collective and Individual Migration
Hyeontae Jeong
Brown University
hyeontae_jeong@brown.edu

Machine Learning of Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transitions during Collective and Individual Migration

The epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) is associated with transitions from stationary tissues towards collective and individual migration during embryonic development, wound healing and tumor progression. For example, activation of EMT transcription factors weaken cell-cell adhesion and promote cell-matrix adhesion with front-back polarity. Recently, partial or hybrid EMT states have been suggested that retain some cell-cell adhesions and act as “leader cells” to coordinate collective migration. However, profiling the functional phenotypes of partial EMT states remains challenging since they are transient and depend on local microenvironmental conditions. Here, we use live cell imaging of EMT fluorescent reporters coupled with machine learning to profile EMT states in mammary epithelial cells after TGF-beta treatment. We found that although we can roughly distinguish between epithelial and mesenchymal states using either cell morphology or fluorescent reporter output as separate methods, classifying cells within partial EMT subgroups remain challenging. By integrating reporter expression with morphology classification, we identify distinct subgroups of partial EMT cells that exhibit unique migration patterns within the monolayer. For instance, under intermediate TGF-beta treatment, larger cells marked with the epithelial marker demonstrate active "leading" migration, while smaller cells are "passively" steered by their neighbors. Moreover, the increase in heterogeneity within neighboring cells, including a rise in 'leader-like' cells, significantly enhances the robustness and dynamics of cell migration. Consequently, the EMT-induced cell population demonstrates stronger and more sustained collective migration. We envision this approach can be applied more broadly to elucidate single cell plasticity across epithelial and mesenchymal states during collective migration in the context of development and disease.

Collective Transitions from Orbiting to Matrix Invasion in 3D Multicellular Spheroids
Jiwon Kim
Brown University
jiwon_kim1@brown.edu

Collective Transitions from Orbiting to Matrix Invasion in 3D Multicellular Spheroids

Coordinated cell rotation along a curved matrix interface can sculpt epithelial tissues into spherical morphologies. Subsequently, radially-oriented invasion of multicellular strands or branches can occur by local remodeling of the confining matrix. These symmetry-breaking transitions emerge from the dynamic reciprocity between cells and matrix, but remain poorly understood. Here, we show that epithelial cell spheroids collectively transition from circumferential orbiting to radial invasion via bi-directional interactions with the surrounding matrix curvature. Initially, spheroids exhibit an ellipsoidal shape but become rounded as orbiting occurs. However, cells gradually reorient from coordinated rotation towards outward strand invasion due to the accumulation of contractile tractions at discrete sites. Remarkably, the initial ellipsoid morphology predicts subsequent invasion of 2-4 strands roughly aligned with the major axis. We then perturb collective migration using osmotic pressure, showing that orbiting can be arrested and invasion can be reversed. We also investigate coordinated orbiting in “mosaic” spheroids, showing a small fraction of “leader” cells with weakened cell-cell adhesions can impede collective orbiting but still invade into the matrix. Finally, we establish a minimal self-propelled particle model to elucidate how collective orbiting is mediated by the crosstalk of cell-cell and cell-matrix adhesion along a curved boundary. Altogether, this work elucidates how tissue morphogenesis is governed by the interplay of collective behaviors and the local curvature of the cell-matrix, with relevance for embryonic development and tumor progression.

Bistable dynamics of TAN-NK cells in tumor growth and control of radiotherapy-induced neutropenia in lung cancer treatment

Neutrophils play a crucial role in the innate immune response as a first line of defense in many diseases, including cancer. Tumor-associated neutrophils (TANs) can either promote or inhibit tumor growth in various steps of cancer progression via mutual interactions with cancer cells in a complex tumor microenvironment (TME). In this study, we developed and analyzed mathematical models to investigate the role of natural killer cells (NK cells) and the dynamic transition between N1 and N2 TAN phenotypes in killing cancer cells through key signaling networks and how adjuvant therapy with radiation can be used in combination to increase anti-tumor efficacy. We examined the complex immune-tumor dynamics among N1/N2 TANs, NK cells, and tumor cells, communicating through key extracellular mediators (Transforming growth factor (TGF-beta), Interferon gamma (IFN-gamma)) and intracellular regulation in the apoptosis signaling network. We developed several tumor prevention strategies to eradicate tumors, including combination (IFN-gamma, exogenous NK, TGF-βinhibitor) therapy and optimally-controlled ionizing radiation in a complex TME. Using this model, we investigated the fundamental mechanism of radiation-induced changes in the TME and the impact of internal and external immune composition on the tumor cell fate and their response to different treatment schedules.

Glioma Infiltration between Astrocytes and Blood Vessels

"Gliomas are highly lethal tumors, often leading to patient mortality within one year of diagnosis. A major challenge in treating gliomas is their extensive invasion, which allows tumor cells to evade surgical resection, chemotherapy, and radiation therapy. Glioma cells migrate along blood vessels, squeezing through narrow spaces between astrocytic endfeet and blood vessels, while reducing their volume by up to 33%. This volume regulation is closely linked to ion channels, particularly involving chloride (Cl-) and calcium (Ca2+) ions. In this study, we focused on the interactions between these ions and how they contribute to volume regulation in glioma cells. By elucidating the mechanisms underlying this process, we aim to identify strategies to inhibit glioma cell migration, thereby preventing tumor metastasis. Additionally, we investigated how drug interventions could further inhibit this migratory behavior, offering potential therapeutic approaches to limit glioma progression."
